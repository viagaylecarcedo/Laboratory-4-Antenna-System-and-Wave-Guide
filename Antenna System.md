# 📡 Antenna System Laboratory Experiment

## Introduction

An antenna is a metallic device (transducer) that converts electrical currents into electromagnetic waves for transmitting signals, and vice versa for receiving them. It serves as the interface between radio transmitters/receivers and free space, enabling wireless communication systems such as mobile phones, television broadcasting, radio communication, and satellite links.

The performance of an antenna depends on several factors including its geometric design, resonant frequency, and operating wavelength. Since electromagnetic waves have specific wavelengths, antennas must be designed with physical dimensions related to these wavelengths to maximize radiation efficiency and signal transfer.

Antennas come in many forms, ranging from simple dipoles to complex directional arrays. Each type of antenna has different characteristics such as gain, directivity, bandwidth, and radiation pattern that influence communication performance.

# Procedure

### 1. System Setup

1. Prepare the antenna trainer and ensure that all modules are properly connected.
2. Connect the transmitter module to the transmitting antenna.
3. Connect the receiver module to the receiving antenna.
4. Position the antennas facing each other at a short distance.

---

### 2. Signal Transmission

1. Turn on the transmitter module.
2. Observe the transmitted signal through the transmitting antenna.
3. Monitor the received signal on the oscilloscope connected to the receiver module.

---

### 3. Antenna Testing

1. Replace the transmitting antenna with different antenna types.
2. Observe the signal strength received at the receiver.
3. Record the differences in signal quality and directionality.

---

### 4. Interference Test

1. Place an object (such as a magnet or metallic object) between the transmitter and receiver.
2. Observe the change in received signal strength.
3. Record the effect of obstruction on signal propagation.

# Types of Antennas

## 1. Current Probe Antenna

The RF probe antenna is used mainly for diagnostic purposes. It measures electrical current present in another antenna rather than transmitting signals. It acts as a sensor in antenna training systems.

---

## 2. Log Periodic Antenna

The log periodic antenna is a directional antenna capable of operating across a wide frequency range. Unlike a traditional Yagi antenna, it maintains relatively constant performance over multiple frequencies.

---

## 3. Loop Antenna

The loop antenna is sensitive to magnetic fields and consists of looped conductive traces that act as inductive elements. This antenna is often used for receiving low to medium frequency signals.

---

## 4. Hertz Antenna

The Hertz antenna is one of the most fundamental antenna designs. Named after Heinrich Hertz, it demonstrates the basic principles of electromagnetic radiation and resonance.

---

## 5. Rhombus Antenna

The rhombus antenna is a traveling-wave antenna characterized by its diamond-shaped geometry. It is commonly used in long-distance communication due to its broadband performance.

---

## 6. Yagi-Uda Simple 5 Element Antenna

The Yagi-Uda antenna is one of the most widely used directional antennas. It consists of:

* Reflector
* Driven Element
* Director Elements

These elements focus radio signals into a narrow beam, increasing signal strength in one direction.

---

## 7. Yagi-Uda Folded Dipole (3 Element)

This antenna uses a folded dipole as the driven element, which improves impedance matching and allows easier connection with transmission lines.

---

## 8. Yagi-Uda Folded Dipole (5 Element)

Adding more directors increases antenna gain and directivity, allowing the antenna to transmit signals farther and receive weaker signals.

---

## 9. Yagi-Uda Simple 7 Element Antenna

This antenna configuration increases directivity even further by adding additional director elements.

---

## 10. Phase Array λ/2 Antenna

A phased array antenna consists of multiple antenna elements whose signals are controlled with specific phase differences. This allows electronic steering of the radiation beam.

---

## 11. Phase Array λ/4 Antenna

This antenna uses quarter wavelength elements to reduce physical size while maintaining similar operational properties.

---

## 12. Antenna Kit A and B

These components serve as mounting bases or feed point connectors used to attach various antenna elements during experiments.

---

## 13. Broadside Array Antenna

A broadside array radiates electromagnetic waves perpendicular to the antenna axis. This configuration produces a narrow radiation beam and is commonly used in directional communication systems.

---

## 14. Combined Collinear Array Antenna

A collinear antenna array consists of multiple dipole antennas connected end-to-end. This configuration increases signal gain without producing extreme directionality.

---

## 15. Simple Dipole Antenna

The dipole antenna is the simplest and most widely used antenna type. It consists of two equal conductive rods and operates most efficiently at half the wavelength of the signal.

---

## 16. Simple Dipole 3/2λ Antenna

This antenna is a longer variation of the dipole antenna, providing additional gain due to its increased length.

---

## 17. Folded Dipole λ/2 Antenna

A folded dipole antenna improves impedance matching and produces a broader bandwidth compared to a standard dipole antenna.

---

## 18. Ground Plane Antenna

A ground plane antenna uses a conductive surface as a reflector that enhances radiation efficiency.

---

## 19. Slot Antenna

A slot antenna consists of a narrow opening cut into a conductive surface. Radio frequency energy applied across the slot produces electromagnetic radiation.

---

## 20. Zeppelin Antenna

The Zeppelin antenna is a historic antenna design used in early airship communications. It is known for its simplicity and long-wire structure.

# Summary of Findings and Results

### 1. Transmitter and Receiver Setup

The transmitter and receiver were positioned facing each other at a short distance using the antenna trainer system. The transmitting antenna emitted an electromagnetic signal that was received by the receiving antenna.

### 2. Signal Behavior

During the experiment, it was observed that:

* Signal strength depends on antenna alignment.
* Directional antennas produce stronger signals when properly oriented.
* Obstructions between antennas reduce signal strength.

# Observations

The experimental setup demonstrated a simple wireless communication system.

Key observations include:

* The transmitted signal strength was relatively low due to the simple setup.
* Proper alignment of antennas was required to achieve clear signal reception.
* Placing an object between the transmitter and receiver weakened the received signal.
* Signal degradation occurred when electromagnetic waves were partially obstructed.

---

# Conclusions

After analyzing the experiment, the following conclusions were made:

* Antennas serve as essential devices that convert electrical signals into electromagnetic waves for wireless communication.
* Different antenna types exhibit different characteristics such as gain, bandwidth, and radiation patterns.
* Directional antennas such as Yagi-Uda arrays provide stronger signal transmission compared to simple dipole antennas.
* Increasing the number of antenna elements improves antenna gain and directionality.
* Proper alignment between transmitting and receiving antennas is crucial for efficient communication.
* Physical obstacles between antennas can significantly affect signal propagation and reception.
* Understanding antenna resonance and wavelength relationships is important for efficient antenna design and operation.
